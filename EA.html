Equality Act

The Equality Act (brought about in 2010) is a significant piece of legislation in the United Kingdom that aims to protect individuals from discrimination and promote equality. The Act refines and strengthens previous anti-discrimination laws, providing a strong framework for confronting various forms of discrimination based on protected characteristics such as age and disability. The Equality Act consolidates and simplifies existing laws, making it easier for individuals to understand their rights and for organizations to comply with their legal obligations. It covers a wide range of areas, including employment, education, housing, and the provision of goods and services. The Act also establishes the Equality and Human Rights Commission (EHRC) as the body responsible for enforcing the legislation and promoting equality across society. Overall, the Equality Act plays a crucial role in fostering a more inclusive and equitable society in the UK.

All groups protected by the Equality Act:

  • Age
  • Disability
  • Gender
  • Race
  • Religion or belief
  • Sexual orientation
  • Pregnancy

An example of when the Equality Act may be used is when a person is treated less favorably due to a protected characteristic. For instance, if a woman is denied a job due to her pregnancy, this would be considered direct discrimination.

Direct and Indirect Discrimination

Direct discrimination occurs when someone is treated less favorably because of a protected characteristic. Indirect discrimination happens when a policy given by an employer applies to everyone but disadvantages people with a protected characteristic.
